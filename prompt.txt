1. System Overview & Vision
Overview

The system is a multi-vendor rental management platform designed to enable customers to rent physical products online while allowing vendors to maintain full control over their inventory, approvals, and fulfillment. Unlike traditional e-commerce systems that focus on one-time product sales, this platform is built specifically for time-based rentals, where duration, availability, and responsibility play a critical role.

The platform supports multiple independent vendors, each managing their own products, pricing, and rental operations. Customers interact with a single unified marketplace, but behind the scenes the system ensures vendor isolation, accurate accounting, and operational clarity.

This system is designed to operate continuously (24×7) and handle real-world rental complexities such as overlapping bookings, vendor approvals, refunds, and legal invoicing.

Vision

The vision of this system is to create a trust-driven, scalable, and transparent rental ecosystem that balances customer convenience with vendor control.

The platform aims to:

Provide customers with a simple and intuitive rental experience, from discovery to payment and order tracking.

Enable vendors to confidently rent out physical assets by retaining approval authority and operational control.

Ensure financial and legal correctness through secure payments, verified transactions, and vendor-specific invoicing.

Prevent operational failures such as double bookings, fake payments, and inventory conflicts.

Support future growth into a full ERP-grade rental platform capable of handling large vendor networks, analytics, and advanced rental rules.

How This System Differs from Traditional E-Commerce

Traditional e-commerce platforms are designed around ownership transfer—once a product is paid for, it is sold and removed from inventory. In contrast, this rental platform is built around temporary usage, where products return to inventory after a defined time period.

Key differences include:

Time-based transactions instead of quantity-based sales

Vendor approval before fulfillment

Temporary inventory locking instead of permanent stock reduction

Rental lifecycle management instead of simple order completion

Ongoing responsibility for physical assets

Because of these differences, the system requires more structured workflows, stronger validations, and a clearer separation of responsibilities between customers, vendors, and the platform.

Core System Objective

The primary objective of the system is to convert customer rental intent into a legally accountable, vendor-approved rental transaction while maintaining transparency, security, and operational reliability.

To achieve this, the system:

Separates browsing, payment, approval, and fulfillment into clear stages

Treats the backend as the single source of truth

Uses vendor-wise order and invoice management

Enforces strict availability and approval rules

Long-Term Perspective

The system is intentionally designed with future scalability in mind. While it can begin as a localized or single-category rental platform, its architecture supports:

Growth to hundreds of vendors

Multiple rental categories

Advanced pricing and duration models

Analytics and reporting

Integration with logistics and compliance systems

Ultimately, the platform is envisioned as a professional-grade rental management solution rather than a simple marketplace.

One-line summary

This system is a multi-vendor, time-based rental platform that prioritizes trust, control, and transparency while delivering a seamless rental experience for customers and operational confidence for vendors.



2. Core Philosophy & Design Principles
Purpose of the Core Philosophy

The core philosophy of this rental management system defines how trust is established, how responsibility is enforced, and how operational risks are managed across customers, vendors, and the platform.

Because the system deals with physical assets, time-bound usage, and financial liability, it must follow stricter rules than traditional e-commerce platforms. Every workflow in the system—browsing, payment, approval, fulfillment, and completion—is governed by these principles.

These principles are foundational and must not be bypassed or weakened at any stage.

Payment First, Fulfillment Never Without Validation

The system enforces a payment-first model, meaning customers must complete payment before any rental order is created.

However, payment alone does not guarantee rental activation.

Payment serves only one purpose:

To confirm genuine customer intent

Actual fulfillment depends on additional system checks, including:

Product-specific verification requirements

Vendor approval rules

Inventory availability

This separation ensures that money confirms seriousness, while trust and responsibility are established separately.

Conditional Approval Based on Verification Requirements

Not all rental products carry the same level of risk.
Therefore, the system supports two approval models, determined at the product or service level.

Verification-Based Approval (Manual Approval)

This approval mode is used for high-value, regulated, or identity-sensitive rentals, such as:

Vehicles

Professional or industrial equipment

Restricted-access assets

Services requiring legal or identity verification

For these rentals:

Customers must upload specific documents (e.g., ID proof, license, agreements).

After payment verification, the order enters a Pending Vendor Approval state.

Vendors manually review submitted documents.

The rental becomes active only after vendor approval.

This model protects vendors legally and ensures responsible asset handover.

Automatic Approval (No Verification Required)

This approval mode is used for low-risk or easily replaceable items, such as:

Basic electronics

Accessories

Non-regulated consumer products

For these rentals:

No documents are required.

After payment verification, the system automatically approves the rental.

The rental becomes active immediately without vendor intervention.

This model improves user experience and reduces operational overhead for vendors.

Approval Decision Logic

Each product or service includes a verification requirement setting, defined by the vendor or enforced by the platform administrator.

After payment verification, the system evaluates this setting and automatically routes the order through:

Manual verification flow, or

Automatic approval flow

This ensures consistent behavior without manual decision-making at runtime.

Backend as the Single Source of Truth

All critical decisions are made by the backend system, including:

Payment validation

Order creation

Approval routing

Inventory locking

Status transitions

The frontend:

Displays information

Collects user input

Reflects backend decisions

It never determines order validity, payment success, or rental activation.

This prevents fraud, race conditions, and inconsistent system states.

Clear Separation of System Stages

The system strictly separates the rental lifecycle into defined stages:

Intent – Browsing, configuration, cart

Commitment – Payment verification

Validation – Auto-approval or vendor verification

Fulfillment – Rental becomes active

Closure – Completion, rejection, or refund

No stage can be skipped or merged.
This enforces clarity, accountability, and auditability.

Vendor Control and Legal Responsibility

Vendors always retain control over their assets.

The system ensures:

Vendors approve rentals where verification is required.

Vendors see and manage only their own orders.

Vendors are never forced into automatic fulfillment for high-risk products.

This protects vendors from:

Legal disputes

Asset misuse

Identity fraud

Operational conflicts

Vendor-Wise Accounting and Financial Clarity

All financial records are maintained per vendor, including:

Rental orders

Invoices

Taxes

Deposits

Refunds

Even if a customer rents from multiple vendors in one checkout, the system internally:

Splits orders vendor-wise

Generates separate invoices

Maintains isolated accounting trails

This ensures legal compliance and clean financial reporting.

Inventory Protection and Conflict Prevention

Inventory in a rental system is time-bound, not quantity-bound.

The system enforces:

Availability checks before payment

Inventory locking only after payment verification

Strict prevention of overlapping rentals

Automatic inventory release on rejection or refund

This guarantees reliable availability for both vendors and customers.

Design for Scale and Real-World Operation

These principles ensure the system can scale without breaking trust or operations.

They support:

Growth in vendors and products

Mixed approval models

High transaction volume

Future features such as extensions, penalties, and analytics

The system is designed for real-world rental businesses, not just theoretical workflows.

One-Line Philosophy Summary

Payment confirms intent, verification confirms trust, and the system enforces responsibility through structured, rule-driven workflows.


3. User Roles & Access Control
Purpose of Role-Based Design

In a multi-vendor rental platform, role separation is critical.
Different users interact with the system in fundamentally different ways, and unrestricted access would create security risks, operational conflicts, and legal issues.

This system is designed around strict role-based access control (RBAC), ensuring that every user:

Sees only what they are allowed to see

Performs only actions appropriate to their role

Is isolated from data that does not belong to them

Access control is enforced at the system level, not just at the user interface.

Primary User Roles

The platform defines three primary roles:

Customer (End User)

Vendor

Administrator (Admin)

Each role has clearly defined responsibilities, permissions, and limitations.

Customer (End User)
Role Definition

A customer is an individual or organization that uses the platform to rent products or services for a limited period.

Customers interact primarily with the marketplace and checkout flows.

Customer Capabilities

Customers are allowed to:

Browse available rental products

Filter products by category, attributes, and duration

View product details and pricing

Select rental period and configuration

Add items to cart

Apply coupons or discounts (if available)

Complete online payment

Upload required documents (when applicable)

Track rental order status

View rental history and invoices

Receive notifications related to orders and approvals

Customer Restrictions

Customers are not allowed to:

Activate rentals themselves

Bypass vendor approval or verification rules

Modify rental orders after payment

Access vendor dashboards or reports

View or interact with other customers’ or vendors’ data

These restrictions ensure fairness, security, and consistent rental operations.

Vendor
Role Definition

A vendor is a business entity or individual that owns rental assets and is responsible for fulfilling rentals.

Vendors are accountable for:

Asset condition

Legal compliance

Handover and return processes

Vendor Capabilities

Vendors are allowed to:

Create and manage their own products

Define pricing and rental rules for their products

Specify whether products require document verification

View incoming rental orders

Review customer details and submitted documents

Approve or reject rental requests

Manage active rentals

Mark rentals as completed

Generate and view invoices for their orders

Access business-specific reports and analytics

Vendor Restrictions

Vendors are not allowed to:

View or manage products belonging to other vendors

Access platform-wide data or analytics

Modify system-wide settings

Override payment or refund rules

Access admin-level controls

Vendor data isolation is strictly enforced to maintain privacy and competitive integrity.

Administrator (Admin)
Role Definition

The administrator represents the platform owner or operator and is responsible for governance, configuration, and oversight of the entire system.

Admins do not participate in day-to-day rental fulfillment but ensure the system operates correctly and fairly.

Admin Capabilities

Admins are allowed to:

View all users, vendors, and rental orders

Manage vendor accounts and onboarding

Define and manage product categories

Configure attributes and variants

Define rental periods and pricing rules

Monitor payments and refunds

Access platform-wide reports and analytics

Enforce platform policies and compliance

Publish or unpublish products if necessary

Admin Restrictions

Admins typically:

Do not fulfill rentals themselves

Do not interfere in vendor operations unless required

Follow audit and compliance processes for overrides

This separation ensures transparency and accountability.

Access Control Enforcement

Role-based access control is enforced at multiple levels:

Authentication level – determines who the user is

Authorization level – determines what the user can do

Data access level – determines what data the user can see

The system ensures that:

Vendors can access only their own products, orders, and invoices

Customers can access only their own rentals and payments

Admins have visibility across the platform but operate under governance rules

Why Strict Access Control Is Essential

Strict role-based access control:

Prevents data leaks and misuse

Protects vendor business interests

Ensures legal compliance

Maintains platform trust

Enables safe scaling to many vendors and users

Without this structure, the platform would be vulnerable to operational and legal risks.

One-Line Summary

Clear role separation ensures that customers rent, vendors fulfill, and administrators govern—without overlap, confusion, or risk.

4. Product & Rental Model
Purpose of the Product Model

The product model is the foundation of the entire rental system.
Unlike traditional e-commerce platforms where products are sold and removed from inventory, this system treats products as rentable assets that are temporarily assigned to customers and then returned to availability.

The product and rental model is designed to reflect real-world rental business operations, where ownership never changes, only usage does.

Products as Rentable Assets

In this system, a product represents a physical or service-based asset that can be rented for a specific time period.

Key characteristics of rental products:

Products are owned by vendors, not the platform.

Products remain in the system after rentals are completed.

Products can be rented multiple times over their lifetime.

Availability depends on time slots, not just quantity.

Ownership never transfers to the customer.
The system only grants temporary usage rights.

Vendor Ownership and Responsibility

Each product is linked to exactly one vendor.

This ensures:

Clear accountability for asset condition

Vendor-specific pricing and policies

Vendor-specific invoicing and accounting

Isolation of inventory and orders

Vendors are fully responsible for:

Product accuracy

Pricing correctness

Rental conditions

Approval requirements

Asset handover and return

The platform does not take ownership of vendor products.

Product Categories

Products are organized into categories to support:

Browsing and discovery

Filtering and search

Reporting and analytics

Policy enforcement (e.g., category-based rules)

Categories may represent:

Product type (electronics, furniture, vehicles)

Industry verticals

Usage context

Categories are typically managed by administrators to maintain consistency across the platform.

Rental-Based Pricing Model

Pricing in this system is time-dependent, not quantity-dependent.

Each product defines:

A base rental price

Pricing per rental duration unit (hour, day, week, month, etc.)

Price calculation depends on:

Selected rental period

Duration length

Product-specific pricing rules

This model supports:

Short-term rentals

Long-term rentals

Flexible pricing strategies

Quantity affects pricing only if multiple units are available, but duration remains the primary factor.

Product Types: Goods and Services

The system supports two fundamental product types:

Physical Goods

These are tangible assets that are rented, such as:

Electronics

Equipment

Furniture

Vehicles

Physical goods:

Have availability constraints

May require verification

Must be returned after rental

Service-Type Products

Service-type products represent non-physical charges related to rentals, such as:

Security deposits

Down payments

Delivery or pickup charges

Warranty fees

Late penalties

Service products:

Do not require availability checks

Are added to orders and invoices like normal products

Follow the same accounting and tax rules

Treating services as products ensures accounting consistency and avoids special-case logic.

Product Availability Concept

Product availability is not static.

Availability depends on:

Existing rentals

Rental time overlaps

Quantity (if applicable)

Product variants

A product may be:

Available for one time range

Unavailable for another

The system must always evaluate availability in the context of time, not just stock count.

Verification Requirement at Product Level

Each product can define whether it:

Requires document verification

Can be auto-approved

This setting determines:

Whether vendor approval is mandatory

Whether documents must be uploaded

Whether rentals activate automatically

This allows different products to follow different approval flows within the same platform.

Why This Model Is Critical

This product and rental model ensures:

Accurate representation of real rental businesses

Protection of vendor assets

Flexible pricing strategies

Clean separation between assets and services

Scalability across categories and industries

Without this model, the system would behave like a sales platform, which would fail for rentals.

One-Line Summary

Products in this system are vendor-owned, time-bound rental assets priced by duration, governed by availability, and designed for repeated use rather than one-time sale.


5. Attributes, Variants & Configuration
Purpose of Attributes and Variants

In real-world rental businesses, a single product often exists in multiple configurations.
These configurations may differ in appearance, capacity, specification, or model, even though they represent the same base product.

The attributes and variants system allows the platform to:

Represent real product diversity accurately

Maintain clean inventory and pricing logic

Prevent confusion during booking and fulfillment

Attributes: Defining Product Characteristics

An attribute represents a definable characteristic of a product.

Common examples include:

Brand

Color

Size

Model

Capacity

Version

Attributes are defined centrally (typically by administrators) to ensure consistency across the platform. Once defined, attributes can be reused across multiple products and categories.

This prevents duplicate or inconsistent definitions and supports standardized filtering and reporting.

Attribute Values

Each attribute consists of a set of attribute values.

Examples:

Color: Black, White, Silver

Size: Small, Medium, Large

Model: Basic, Pro, Premium

Attribute values are used to create concrete product configurations that customers can select during the rental process.

Variants: Concrete Product Configurations

A variant is a specific combination of attribute values that represents an actual rentable configuration.

For example:

Laptop → Brand: Dell, Model: XPS, RAM: 16GB

Camera → Brand: Canon, Lens Type: Wide Angle

Each variant:

Represents a real, physical configuration

May have its own availability

May have its own pricing

Is treated as a distinct rentable unit for booking purposes

Variants ensure that the system knows exactly what is being rented.

Configuration Selection During Rental

If a product has variants, the customer must explicitly select a configuration before proceeding with rental.

This ensures:

Accurate pricing

Correct availability checks

Proper vendor fulfillment

No ambiguity during handover

The system does not allow customers to rent a configurable product without selecting required attributes.

Impact on Pricing

Variants may influence pricing in different ways:

Some configurations may be more expensive

Some variants may include additional features

Pricing may differ based on capacity or model

The system must calculate rental pricing after configuration selection, ensuring the final price reflects the exact variant being rented.

Impact on Availability

Availability is evaluated at the variant level, not just the product level.

This allows:

One variant to be rented while another remains available

Accurate prevention of overlapping rentals

Better inventory utilization

For example, a product with three color variants may have one color rented while others are free.

Relationship with Verification Requirements

Verification rules may also be applied at the variant level.

For instance:

A high-end variant may require document verification

A basic variant may allow auto-approval

This provides vendors with fine-grained control over risk management.

Why Attributes and Variants Matter

Without attributes and variants:

Inventory tracking becomes inaccurate

Pricing becomes ambiguous

Vendor fulfillment becomes error-prone

Customers may receive the wrong configuration

This system ensures clarity for all parties by enforcing explicit configuration selection and structured variant management.

One-Line Summary

Attributes define what a product can be, variants define what is actually rented, and configuration ensures accuracy in pricing, availability, and fulfillment.


6. Rental Period & Pricing Logic
Purpose of Rental Period Design

In a rental system, time is the primary unit of value.
Unlike sales platforms where pricing depends mainly on quantity, this system bases all pricing, availability, and responsibility on rental duration.

The rental period and pricing logic ensure that:

Customers are charged fairly based on usage time

Vendors maintain control over asset availability

Inventory conflicts are avoided

Financial calculations remain consistent and auditable

Rental Period Definition

Every rental transaction must include a clearly defined rental period, consisting of:

Start date and time

End date and time

Duration unit (hour, day, week, month, etc.)

A rental cannot exist without a valid rental period.
This information is mandatory for all pricing and availability calculations.

Duration Units

The system supports multiple duration units to accommodate different rental models, such as:

Hourly rentals (short-term usage)

Daily rentals (common consumer rentals)

Weekly or monthly rentals (long-term usage)

Duration units are defined at the platform level and applied consistently across products.

Time-Based Pricing Model

Pricing in the system is calculated based on time, not ownership.

Key principles:

Each product or variant defines pricing per duration unit

The total price is derived from the selected rental period

Longer durations may follow different pricing strategies

Examples:

Linear pricing (fixed rate per day)

Tiered pricing (discounts for longer durations)

Flat-rate pricing for specific periods

The pricing model is flexible enough to support various vendor strategies while maintaining system consistency.

Pricing Calculation Sequence

To ensure accuracy, pricing is calculated in a defined order:

Product and variant selection

Rental period selection

Duration calculation

Base rental price determination

Additional charges (if applicable)

Discounts or coupons (if applicable)

Final pricing is always determined by the backend to prevent manipulation or inconsistencies.

Handling Partial Periods

The system must clearly define how partial rental periods are handled.

Examples:

Rounding up to the nearest hour or day

Charging pro-rata rates

Enforcing minimum rental durations

These rules are standardized to prevent confusion and disputes.

Relationship Between Pricing and Availability

Rental pricing and availability are closely linked.

Availability is evaluated for the exact selected time period

Pricing is calculated only after availability is validated

Changes to rental dates automatically trigger price recalculation

This ensures that customers always see accurate and consistent information.

Impact on Inventory and Order Lifecycle

The rental period directly affects:

When inventory is locked

How long an asset is unavailable

When it becomes available again

Incorrect handling of rental periods would result in:

Overlapping rentals

Incorrect pricing

Vendor disputes

Therefore, rental period validation is a critical system responsibility.

Support for Extensions and Future Enhancements

The system is designed to support future features such as:

Rental extensions

Early returns

Late fees or penalties

These features rely on the same rental period logic, ensuring long-term scalability.

Why Rental Period Logic Is Central to the System

Without strict rental period logic:

Pricing becomes arbitrary

Availability becomes unreliable

Trust between customers and vendors breaks down

Time-based logic ensures fairness, predictability, and operational stability.

One-Line Summary

Rental periods define the value, availability, and responsibility of every transaction, making time the core currency of the system.

This phase focuses entirely on:

Exploration

Comparison

Decision-making

It intentionally avoids any form of reservation or blocking to keep the system flexible and accurate.

Entry Point to the Marketplace

After authentication, customers enter a unified marketplace where products from multiple vendors are displayed together in a consistent format.

From the customer’s perspective:

The platform appears as a single rental marketplace

Vendor complexity is hidden

Pricing and availability are clearly presented

Behind the scenes, each product remains tied to its respective vendor.

Product Listing View

The product listing page presents rental products in a card-based or grid layout, displaying:

Product image

Product name

Base rental price (per duration unit)

Availability indicator

Key attributes (if relevant)

The goal of this view is quick scanning and comparison, allowing customers to identify relevant options efficiently.

Filtering and Search

To help customers navigate a potentially large catalog, the system provides robust filtering and search capabilities.

Filters may include:

Product category

Attributes (brand, color, size, model)

Rental duration options

Price range

Availability status

Search allows customers to quickly locate specific products or keywords.

These tools improve usability without impacting inventory or order logic.

Availability Indicators

Availability indicators provide guidance, not guarantees.

They show:

Whether a product is generally available

Whether certain time periods may be unavailable

At this stage:

Availability is informational

No inventory is locked

Final availability is always revalidated later

This prevents misleading customers while maintaining system flexibility.

Pricing Display Strategy

Prices shown during browsing represent:

Base rental rates

Typical duration pricing

Final pricing may vary based on:

Exact rental period

Product configuration

Additional charges or discounts

This approach balances transparency with accuracy.

Wishlist and Saving for Later

Customers may save products to a wishlist for future reference.

Wishlist functionality:

Does not affect availability

Does not reserve inventory

Is purely a convenience feature

Read-Only Nature of Browsing

A critical design principle of the discovery phase is that it is read-only.

During browsing:

No orders are created

No inventory is reserved

No financial records are generated

This ensures:

Accurate availability for all users

No artificial stock shortages

A fair browsing experience

Transition to Rental Intent

Rental intent begins only when a customer:

Opens a product detail page

Selects a rental period and configuration

Until then, the system treats browsing as exploratory.

Why This Flow Matters

A poorly designed discovery flow can:

Confuse customers

Misrepresent availability

Create false expectations

This system ensures that customers can explore freely while protecting vendors from inaccurate reservations.

One-Line Summary

The discovery flow enables effortless exploration and comparison while deliberately avoiding commitments, ensuring accurate availability and a fair experience for all users.

The product detail and configuration stage marks the transition from casual browsing to rental intent.
At this stage, the customer begins making specific choices that determine pricing, availability, and fulfillment, but without yet committing to a rental.

This stage is designed to gather accurate rental requirements while still protecting inventory and vendor operations.

Product Detail Page Overview

The product detail page provides comprehensive information about a rental product, including:

Product images and description

Key specifications and attributes

Base rental pricing

Supported rental duration units

Vendor-related information (where appropriate)

Availability guidance

This page allows customers to fully understand the product before proceeding.

Rental Period Selection

Customers must select:

Rental start date and time

Rental end date and time

This selection defines:

The intended usage duration

Preliminary pricing

Tentative availability

The system immediately validates:

Logical date selection (end after start)

Minimum and maximum rental durations

Supported duration units

However, inventory is not locked at this stage.

Product Configuration Selection

If a product has attributes and variants, the customer must select the required configuration.

This includes:

Choosing attribute values (such as model, size, color)

Selecting optional add-ons or service components

Configuration selection ensures:

Precise pricing

Correct availability evaluation

Accurate vendor fulfillment

The system does not allow progression without completing mandatory configuration steps.

Tentative Pricing Calculation

Once rental period and configuration are selected, the system calculates a tentative rental price.

This price:

Reflects the selected duration

Accounts for the chosen variant

Excludes final adjustments such as taxes, deposits, or coupons (if applicable)

The tentative price helps customers make informed decisions without creating financial commitments.

Availability Validation at Detail Level

At this stage, availability checks are:

Informational

Non-blocking

The system may indicate:

Fully available

Partially available

Limited availability

Final availability validation always occurs later during checkout.

Handling Verification Requirements

If the selected product or variant requires document verification:

The system clearly informs the customer

Required document types are displayed

Customers are made aware that approval is mandatory

This transparency prevents confusion after payment.

Transition to Cart

After completing rental period and configuration selection, the customer may add the product to the cart.

At this point:

Rental intent is captured

No inventory is reserved

No order is created

No payment occurs

The cart simply stores the customer’s selected options for later review.

Why Inventory Is Not Locked Here

Locking inventory at the product detail stage would:

Block availability for other users unnecessarily

Lead to abandoned reservations

Create operational inefficiencies

Therefore, inventory locking is intentionally deferred until after payment verification.

Design Benefits of This Flow

This structured approach ensures:

Accurate rental configuration

Informed customer decisions

Protection against premature inventory locking

Smooth transition into checkout

One-Line Summary

The product detail and configuration flow captures precise rental intent while keeping inventory flexible and vendor operations protected.


9. Cart & Checkout Philosophy
Purpose of the Cart in a Rental System

In this rental platform, the cart is designed as an intent review space, not a commitment mechanism.
Its primary purpose is to allow customers to review, modify, and confirm their rental choices before proceeding to payment.

Unlike traditional e-commerce carts, the rental cart does not represent ownership or reservation.

Cart as an Intent Buffer

The cart stores:

Selected products

Chosen rental periods

Selected configurations and variants

Tentative pricing

Optional add-ons or services

However, the cart does not:

Create orders

Reserve inventory

Block availability

Generate financial records

This ensures the system remains fair and responsive under high usage.

Multi-Vendor Cart Support

The cart can contain products from multiple vendors.

From the customer’s perspective:

The cart appears unified

Checkout is seamless

Internally:

The system tracks vendor ownership per item

Vendor separation is preserved for later order creation

This approach simplifies the customer experience while maintaining operational correctness.

Price Revalidation in Cart

When items are added to the cart, the system:

Recalculates tentative pricing

Revalidates rental periods

Updates totals dynamically

Any changes to:

Rental dates

Configuration

Quantity

Trigger immediate price updates, ensuring transparency.

Coupons and Discounts

The cart is the appropriate stage to apply:

Coupons

Promotional discounts

Campaign-based offers

Applying discounts before payment:

Ensures customer clarity

Prevents post-payment disputes

Maintains clean accounting

Coupons affect pricing but do not change availability rules.

Transition from Cart to Checkout

Proceeding to checkout represents the customer’s intent to commit, but still does not finalize the rental.

At checkout:

Customer details are confirmed

Delivery or pickup options are selected

Billing information is collected

The system performs stricter validations at this stage.

Checkout as a Validation Gateway

Checkout serves as a validation gateway between intent and commitment.

During checkout, the system:

Revalidates availability

Recalculates final pricing

Confirms verification requirements

Prepares for payment initiation

If issues are detected, the customer is notified before payment.

Why Inventory Is Still Not Locked

Even at checkout:

Inventory remains unlocked

Final locking occurs only after payment verification

This avoids:

Deadlocks caused by abandoned checkouts

Artificial stock shortages

Poor customer experience

Commitment Begins Only at Payment

The system defines payment initiation and verification as the point where commitment begins.

Before payment:

Everything is reversible

No operational impact occurs

After payment verification:

Orders are created

Inventory is locked

Approval workflows begin

Design Benefits of This Philosophy

This approach:

Prevents inventory abuse

Reduces abandoned reservations

Maintains fairness across users

Keeps vendor operations predictable

One-Line Summary

The cart and checkout process capture and validate customer intent while deliberately delaying commitment until payment is securely verified.


10. Payment System & Verification Flow
Purpose of the Payment System

The payment system is responsible for financial integrity, not business logic.
Its role is to securely collect money, confirm authenticity, and provide verifiable proof of payment to the platform.

In a rental system involving physical assets and refunds, payment verification is critical and cannot rely on frontend signals alone.

Separation of Payment and Rental Logic

The payment gateway:

Handles money transactions

Generates payment confirmations

Ensures secure financial transfer

The rental system:

Decides whether an order is valid

Determines whether a rental is activated

Controls inventory and approval workflows

This separation ensures that:

Financial operations remain secure

Business rules remain enforceable

System logic is not compromised by payment UI behavior

Payment Intent Creation

When a customer proceeds to pay, the backend system creates a payment intent.

The payment intent:

Represents a request to collect a specific amount

A clear lifecycle prevents:

Ambiguous order states

Inventory conflicts

Vendor confusion

Customer mistrust

Each rental order exists in exactly one state at any given time.

Who can take those actions

How inventory and finances are handled

The system uses the following core states to manage rental orders.

Payment Successful

This state indicates:

Payment has been verified by the backend

A rental order has been created

The customer’s intent is financially confirmed

At this stage:

Inventory is provisionally locked

Vendor action may or may not be required

Fulfillment has not yet started

Pending Vendor Approval

This state applies when:

The product requires verification

Manual vendor approval is mandatory

In this state:

The vendor reviews the order

Customer documents (if required) are evaluated

No physical handover occurs

Auto-Approved

This state applies when:

The product does not require verification

Auto-approval is enabled

Vendor intervention is skipped

Rental fulfillment can begin

Rental duration is enforced

Completed

This state indicates:

Rental period has ended

Asset has been returned or access has expired

No further actions are pending

Inventory becomes available again

Financial records remain locked

The order is archived for reporting

Rejected

Inventory release

Customer notification

Refunded

Rental start and end time

Existing active or pending rentals

Unavailable for another

The rental is completed, or

Inventory is released when:

A rental is completed

A rental is rejected by the vendor

A refund is fully processed

This ensures assets return to availability at the correct time.

Inventory is managed at the variant level when applicable.

This ensures:

Accurate tracking of specific configurations

Better utilization of inventory

Reduced fulfillment errors

The system strictly prevents:

Two active or pending rentals from overlapping on the same asset or variant

Manual overrides that would compromise availability integrity

This rule is enforced at the system level.

Without strict inventory rules:

Vendors may face double bookings

Customers may experience cancellations

Platform trust would erode

This design ensures reliability, fairness, and operational stability.
